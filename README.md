# AI Market Formation in Africa

### Owning the AI Transition: Compute, Talent & Market Formation

![HBS Africa Business Conference AI Panel](assets/hbs-africa-panel.jpeg)

---

On March 28, 2026, I moderated a panel at Harvard Business School's 28th Annual Africa 
Business Conference. The session was titled "Owning the AI Transition: Compute, Talent & 
Market Formation in Africa." I was joined by Emmanuel Lubanzadio, Africa Lead at OpenAI; 
Kennith Jackson, SVP AI Solutions at Andela; and Doha Ammour, VP International Business 
Development at N+ONE Data Centers.

The conference theme was "Owning Tomorrow: Africa's Agency in a Shifting World." Last 
year's panel focused on AI at the application layer. This year I wanted to ask a harder 
question: what actually needs to be in place for early AI wins to translate into real 
market formation and sustained economic impact? If 2025 was the year of widespread 
consumer and enterprise adoption, 2026 is when the economic consequences of real scale 
are becoming clear — who captures value, and which companies and countries benefit.

So we talked about the unsexy but essential enablers: compute infrastructure, talent 
systems, and policy frameworks.

---

## How the session was structured

**Act 1: Ground reality.** Where is demand today and where are the friction points? Are 
the use cases people cite actually in production — with budgets and business owners behind 
them — or still in experimentation?

**Act 2: Structural bottlenecks.** What is preventing scale across compute, talent, and 
policy? Not the high-level answer — the specific thing that breaks first.

**Act 3: Debate and future bets.** Where is the narrative wrong? What does Africa 
actually need to own to matter in AI over the next decade?

---

## What the conversation revealed

I went in expecting the sharpest disagreements to be about compute and policy, but there was an interesting convergence around talent at a bottleneck.

**The bottleneck is human infrastructure, not the model.**

All three panelists, from distinct positions on the stack, kept arriving at the same 
place. Compute is being built. Models are accessible. The thing that causes AI projects 
to stay stuck in experimentation is the absence of people who can bridge business goals 
with a technical roadmap — practitioners who can look at a messy legacy system and 
architect a path to production a board can stand behind. That reframes where the 
highest-leverage interventions actually are. The policy conversation fixates on data 
centers and regulatory frameworks. The investment conversation fixates on foundation 
models and applications. Neither unlocks value without the practitioner layer connecting 
them to real enterprise systems.

**Africa's greenfield position is a genuine structural advantage.**

Many African enterprises are still building their core tech stacks, which means they can 
embed AI directly into how systems are designed rather than layering it onto decades of 
technical debt. Safaricom rebuilt M-Pesa's core to be cloud and AI-native. Banks in North 
Africa are compressing lending workflows from days to minutes. That kind of compression 
is structurally harder when you're wrestling infrastructure from a prior era.

**Brain drain is the wrong frame.**

The more useful frame is brain circulation. African talent earning global salaries while 
staying in Lagos, Nairobi, and Cairo — then deploying world-class experience back into 
African enterprises — is a competitive strategy for the continent, not a loss. The 
question is whether the institutions exist to activate that circulation at scale.

**Sovereign AI is a rallying cry that needs stress-testing.**

The idea that African countries need to own their compute, their models, and their data 
pipelines is becoming a common policy position. The panel pushed on whether that is a 
coherent strategy or a distraction from more tractable problems. The honest answer is 
that it depends heavily on the specific country and the specific layer of the stack. 
Continental infrastructure collaboration is likely more realistic than 54 independent 
sovereign AI projects — but the political economy of that coordination is genuinely hard.

---

## Open questions I left with

Africa will provide 85% of the global increase in working-age population by 2050. The 
question the panel kept circling is whether the systems to activate that potential get 
built now or a decade too late. A few threads worth pulling further:

What does a talent infrastructure strategy look like at the continental level, not just 
the company level?

Is there a realistic model for regional compute collaboration that doesn't require 
resolving the full political complexity of the African Union first?

Which African market is genuinely building the conditions for AI market formation — and 
what are they doing that others aren't?
